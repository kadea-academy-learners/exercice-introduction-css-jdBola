Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who saved a billion lives

Dr, Norman Borlaug, third from the left, trains biologists in Mexico on how to increase wheat yields - part of his life-long war on hunger

Here's a time line of Dr. Borlaug's life

  • 1914 - Born in Cresco, Iowa
  • 1933 - Leaves his family's farm to attend University of Minesota, thanks to Depression era program know as the "National Youth Administration".
  • 1935
  • 1937 - Finishes university and takes a job in the US Forestry Service
  • 1938 - Marries wife of 69 years Margret Gibson. Gets laid off due to budget cutt. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, who teaches him about breeding pest-resistent plants.
  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the military after the Pearl Harbor attack, but is rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DTT to control malaria, disinfectants, and other applied science.
  • 1942 - Recieves Ph.D. in Genetics and Plants Pathology
  • 1944 - Rejet a 100% salary increase from Dupont, leaves behiend his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16years, his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.
  • 1945 - Discovers a way to grown wheat twice each season, doubling wheat yields
  • 1953 - crosses a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yeilding American breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizer. It goes on to provide 95% of Mexico's wheat.
  • 1962 - Visits Delhi and brings his high-yielding strains of wheat to the Idian subcontinent in te time to help mitigate mass starvation due to rapidly expanding population
  • 1970 - receives the Nobel Price
  • 1883 - helps seven Africain contries dramatically increase their maize and sorghum yields
  • 1984 - becomes a distinguished professor at Texas A&M University
  • 2005 - states "we will have to double the world food supply by 2050". Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the demand, as we run out of arable land. Says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because "we've been genetically mogifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people were selecting the best breeds."
  • 2009 - dies at the age of 95.
